How pricing works

Quotes are built from actual print effort, not a flat one-size-fits-all rate.

Base estimate structure

Estimates start with a setup fee, then add material cost, machine time, queue tier effects, and any special handling needed for unusual materials or colors.

Why larger and longer prints cost more

Large parts and long prints use more material, occupy the printer for longer, raise failed-print risk, and affect the queue for other students.

Why Economy costs less

Economy jobs receive a 10% discount because they are printed during available idle time with a 7-10 day lead time.

Why rush jobs cost more

Rush work uses schedule flexibility, creates more queue pressure, and increases production risk compared with standard student jobs.

FAQ

Pricing questions, answered clearly

The calculator is an estimate only. Final pricing happens after file review.

What drives the final quote?

Print time, material choice, filament usage, supports, geometry complexity, and deadline urgency all affect pricing after file review.

Why can final pricing change?

Geometry complexity, support material, failed-print risk, tight tolerances, large part size, file repair needs, stock availability, and multi-part jobs can all change the final quote.

What counts as special review?

Prints over 12 hours, unusual materials, fragile features, steep overhangs, split-print assembly needs, and immediate scheduling often require manual review.

When is campus printing still best?

For small, non-urgent jobs, campus printing can be cheapest if a printer is available. UCF Prints becomes more valuable when deadlines, reliability, or queue risk matter.

Why does UCF Prints price large jobs higher?

Longer jobs tie up equipment, raise failed-print risk, and reduce schedule flexibility for other students.

What makes Economy different?

Economy uses a more flexible 7-10 day lead time and gives a straightforward 10% discount on the estimate.
